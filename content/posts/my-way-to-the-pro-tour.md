I played my first Pro Tour this past weekend in Las Vegas, and the experience was beyond all expectation. I finished 19th with an 11-5 record on the weekend (4-2 draft, 7-3 constructed) and lost my win-and-in to Worlds along the way; though it's hard to complain about ending up here, I'd be lying if I said I wasn't at least a bit disappointed not to get there in that last match. I played against some of the best competition I've ever faced: a couple of RC/Spotlight winners, a couple of PT winners, and the reigning World Champion. But in this reflection, I want to go from the very beginning of this run, where the dream honestly started with something of a meme.

## A Hope and a Dreamdew Entrancer

Right before my fiancée (Michelle) and I embarked on a two-month trip to Asia, we registered for the NRG Series Team Trios event in the beginning of November with a friend of ours; since the event was in a suburb of Chicago, it was fairly easy to get to and would probably be a good time anyway. Michelle piloted Mono-Red in Standard (pre-Screaming Nemesis ban), I was on UB Frog in Modern after being inspired by a friend to pick up the deck, and our friend David was in the mines playing Spiderman sealed. Unfortunately, I punted my face off the entire Saturday morning on Frog and we quickly went 1-3, which put us out of contention for prizing. David had brought a Modern deck, I had brought a Standard brew, and Michelle still had her Mono-Red deck she was playing in the morning, so we stuck around to play some ReCQs in the afternoon. 

I think the first thing that you should know about me as a Magic player is that I'm not afraid to register cards/decks that others would find "suboptimal". Some of my earliest competitive experiences, when WotC was taking baby steps towards the Magic: Spotlight Series post-COVID, were with a Grixis Midrange midrange-combo homebrew with [[Hidestugu and Kairi]] and [[Push // Pull]] in Standard around *March of the Machine* (proudly featured in [this Frank Karsten article|https://magic.gg/news/metagame-mentor-murders-at-karlov-manor-standard-at-magiccon-chicago]). During the ensuing Pioneer RCQ season, I spent an endless amount of time daydreaming/testing of an Azorius build of [[Nine Lives]] donation in a meta dominated by Amalia Combo, Izzet Phoenix, and Rakdos Vampires; I could fill another entire essay with the amount of stuff I have to say about this deck, and still regret to this day not being able to bring this creation to an RC.

Anyway, the deck I brought that day was certainly my own creation. In a Standard format where you were allowed to play cards like [[Vivi Ornitier]], [[Proft's Eidetic Memory]], and [[Ouroboroid]], I decided to register this pile of cards:

```deck
Title: Simic Blink
Format: Standard
Source: https://moxfield.com/decks/7EJTECgDk0mitOhjQ8c5Aw

4 Botanical Sanctum
4 Breeding Pool
2 Doppelgang
3 Dreamdew Entrancer
1 Forest
3 Hedge Maze
4 Hide on the Ceiling
2 Into the Flood Maw
3 Island
4 Llanowar Elves
2 Mistrise Village
4 Multiversal Passage
3 Quantum Riddler
1 Restless Vinestalk
3 Spider-Sense
4 Splash Portal
3 Three Steps Ahead
4 Thundertrap Trainer
2 Tishana's Tidebinder
4 Willowrush Verge

Sideboard
1 Aegis Turtle
1 Annul
1 Essence Scatter
1 Ghost Vacuum
2 Heritage Reclamation
1 Into the Flood Maw
1 Negate
1 Pick Your Poison
1 Soul-Guide Lantern
1 Tishana's Tidebinder
1 Ugin, Eye of the Storms
2 Unable to Scream
1 Vivien Reid
```

The deck heavily supports a blink theme by playing 4 [[Splash Portal]] and 4 [[Hide on the Ceiling]]. Both are hits off of [[Thundertrap Trainer]] and generate even more value when blinking Thundertrap Trainer and/or [[Dreamdew Entrancer]] (build-your-own Ancestral Recall), and can also give you some explosive starts scamming [[Quantum Riddler]]: with a dual land T1 into [[Llanowar Elves]], you can scam a Riddler into play T2 with a blue land and Splash Portal. The scam also works with [[Spider-Sense]], though you don't get your second draw and have to bounce your Llanowar Elves to go for that line. In general, Spider-Sense works in the deck not only as solid interaction, but also a synergy piece: bouncing a Thundertrap Trainer or Dreamdew Entrancer for another ETB trigger later feels amazing.

The coolest part of the deck to me lies in the power and flexibility between the synergy pieces. [[Hide on the Ceiling]] and [[Spider-Sense]] are both blink/self-bounce enablers, but together they're actually a one-sided wrath: target all of the creatures/artifacts you want to exile with Hide on the Ceiling, then Spider-Sense the delayed trigger for them to come back. [[Tishana's Tidebinder]] also does the trick when you don't have Spider-Sense for countering the return trigger; I've played games where I have two Hide on the Ceilings in hand and a Tidebinder on board, so I play one targeting my Tidebinder and the other targeting everything I want to exile, then order my Tidebinder to come back first, countering the second return trigger. [[Dreamdew Entrancer]] is also an important piece of the deck: if you aren't under pressure, it can lock one of your creatures down that you might want to bounce with Spider-Sense to draw 2. But if you're dying and there's a big threat hitting you hard, you can just stun it for three turns, buying you a ton of time.

In terms of finishers, I ended up running 2 [[Doppelgang]] because the deck draws so many cards and ends up with a lot of mana when you want to close out the game. There were certainly a lot of options and I could have optimized much more here, but it also did help that you could find Doppelgang off of [[Thundertrap Trainer]]. Finally, the sideboard was a bit of everything, but mostly geared towards hating on decks with [[Vivi Ornitier]] and giving myself a little bit of time against Mono-Red.

To be honest, I did not think this deck was close to the best deck in Standard at that time; I think it was abundantly clear that Vivi-based Izzet decks were objectively the best thing to be doing in the format at the time. That being said, I really enjoyed the lines that you could find with the deck, and I loved the fact that you could play these severely underestimated cards like [[Dreamdew Entrancer]] and [[Hide on the Ceiling]] and put up a strong fight against the meta decks. In addition, I figured that I wouldn't have any real gameplay edge playing the Izzet mirror; I hadn't played a ton of Standard at that point in time, and I'm sure anyone I played would have had more reps in those matchups than me. But by playing this wacky Simic Blink list, I could drag them into the mud on lines that neither of us had much experience playing (maybe even me more than them). This concept of playing decks that you're comfortable piloting as a way to gain equity is one that will resurface later (and I'll share more thoughts on this concept then).

My first match in the ReCQ was against Simic Ouroboroid. It's a decent matchup so long as they don't ramp too aggressively: the version that they were on played Innkeeper's Talent, which was probably good for me as it made their clock a little slower. I ended up winning that match 2-0 on having the [[Hide on the Ceiling]] and [[Spider-Sense]] wrath both times, eating their entire board and leaving them with not a lot to do.

My second opponent in the ReCQ was piloting the villain deck: Izzet Cauldron. Game 1, I'm again able to stabilize and find the [[Hide on the Ceiling]] + [[Tishana's Tidebinder]] wrath, which takes their [[Vivi Ornitier]], [[Fear of Missing Out]], and [[Agatha's Soul Cauldron]] (it also hits artifacts). Once I stick a [[Quantum Riddler]], the game ends swiftly after. For the second game, all of my artifact/enchantment hate comes in: [[Annul]], [[Heritage Reclamation]], [[Vivien Reid]]. I have enough answers for his questions, and eventually stick a [[Vivien Reid]] that he can't deal with, and that engine gets me over the finish line.

Since the ReCQ was small (just 8 people), this gets me into the finals, where my opponent already has an invite and scoops to me for prizing. Invite acquired with this pile of cards!

## I Really Did Not Want to Play Lessons or Landfall in Milwaukee

Fast-forward a few months: I have a great time in Asia not playing a lot of Magic (minus a few weekends of Arena Direct), and TLA and ECL both release while I'm over there. When I arrive back in the US in late January, the Standard meta has mostly settled on two powerful decks as pillars of the format: Izzet Lessons (with [[Artist's Talent]] and [[Monument to Endurance]]) and Simic Cub (with [[Badgermole Cub]] and [[Nature's Rhythm]]). There are also some builds of 4c Elementals and Izzet Spellementals (with [[Sunderflock]] as a clean answer to Cub) floating around from PT ECL as good metagame answers.

I had around a month to figure out what to play for RC Milwaukee (I only had one invite and didn't want to bother flying out to Portland to play a bunch of LCQs on Friday), but the timing crept up on me and I still wasn't sure what I was doing a week out from the event. I'd trialed most of the decks a little on MTGA at this point, but none of them felt particularly good to play. Zevin Faust's Azorius Prison list stood out to me as a list that I thought was interesting (many friends of mine commented that the list felt very "me", whatever that means), and I ended up picking up a bunch of the cards a few days before the RC. After a bit more testing on MTGA though, I realized that there are a lot of small decision points with UW Prison that make it incredibly hard to pilot. There is also very little card flow (besides a couple of [[Enduring Curiosity]]), so you really have to maximize the value of all of your cards. Quite honestly, I felt like this list looked fun to pilot in theory, but in practice would give me lots of opportunities to misplay and punt games away due to my lack of experience with the deck, so I decided against it a couple days before the RC.

At this point, I was really confused and honestly didn't even have too many options in terms of acquiring the cards before the RC. I trawled through decklists on MTGTop8 and MTGGoldfish, doing anything I can to find inspiration on something that I can play. After some vigorous digging, I stumbled across the Boros Dragons list which won the South American RC in Santiago a week or two previous.

I loaded up this list and played a couple of matches on MTGA. I don't remember how the matches went, but I liked how clean many of the lines felt. T1 Momo into T2 Clarion Conqueror/Nova Hellkite, Sarkhan making a Treasure to power out a Magmatic Hellkite on T3; there were many examples where the curveout felt extremely smooth. I had no delusions that this was the best deck in the format, but it did feel extremely powerful on the nut draws and pretty decent post-board as well, with access to a reasonable amount of hate pieces in white as well as lots of card advantage in [[The Legend of Roku]] for grindy games. Quite honestly, I also really liked the fact that the deck encourages you to play spells Main Phase 1 of your turn: [[Momo, Friendly Flier]] and [[Sarkhan, Dragon Ascendant]] both get pumped when your dragons enter, so you're almost always playing out creatures in your first main phase before combat even if they don't have haste. This made sequencing even more straightforward in many spots, which gave me less latitude to punt.

Beyond the gameplay, I had played a lot of Tarkir: Dragonstorm limited; I played the Spotlight Series event in Denver and won 5 collector booster boxes in the Arena Direct (Michelle convinced me to crack one or two of them on the basis that they were "free" since I had won more than my entry back on the others). So upon sorting through those cards, I found exactly 4 mismatched [[Sarkhan, Dragon Ascendant]] and more than enough copies of [[Magmatic Hellkite]] and [[Maelstrom of the Spirit Dragon]], which meant I had most of the list lying around and ready to go. So with most of the cards ready to go, I ended up registering 74 out of the 75 that Joao registered in his winning list, substituting one [[Cavern of Souls]] for [[Multiversal Passage]]. I really dislike [[Multiversal Passage]] in general and often felt like I wanted a [[Cavern of Souls]] (especially post-board against blue decks) in my mana base, so it felt natural to add the extra [[Cavern of Souls]] for 3 total.

## Playing More Standard Than I Signed Up For at RC Milwaukee

Going into the tournament, I felt at ease with the outcome, good or bad. As I told my friends repeatedly that weekend, my goal was actually to "minimize the amount of Standard I would be playing" by registering Boros Dragons, as I was honestly stoked to play the Limited Championship Qualifier on Sunday when I failed to make Day 2. At the end of the day, I had less than 5 matches on the deck I was playing entering the tournament, so I really had no expectations. The day before the event, I played some ECL LCQs for fun and spent some time finding the last few cards for the tournament; my friends picked up a single [[Dragonhawk, Fate's Tempest]] from *The Mana Vault* on their way up and I managed to borrow a clutch set of [[Requisition Raid]]s from a friend, so I ended up finding all the cards I needed.

The tournament went much better than expected. I started 6-0 and immediately qualified for Day 2, squashing my dreams of playing the LCQ on Sunday. I was definitely running hot, but I did not feel like I was playing significantly better than my usual level. That being said, I was playing quite tight, not throwing games by giving my opponents outs and also playing to outs of my own (which I did hit a couple times). Again, the deck also made it easier for me to find the right lines, with the incentivization to play cards Main Phase 1 as I mentioned before. The other benefit of playing an extremely aggressive deck with no card flow in a long, 9-round day was the brevity of rounds. I finished every match with around 15 to 20 minutes left on the round timer, which gave me plenty of time to decompress, stay hydrated, and even pick up lunch/Gatorade to refuel.

I lost my first round of the day at 6-0 in a tight three games to Bant Airbending; the ending sequence had me wondering if there were ways for me to get there that game. I recovered quickly after and ended up finishing 8-1 on the day. The strangest part of the entire experience was the fact that I didn't feel like I was playing beyond myself; it just felt like a good run with good variance. My friends and I had an excellent dinner and a couple rounds of drinks at [a nice supper club|https://maps.app.goo.gl/BjGRCPvq7xfNeqzU8] for some premature celebration, then we ended the day playing a little more ECL Arena Direct and goofing around (some of them even fired a Commander game) before calling it a night. 

Day 2 started off a lot worse for me: I lost badly to 4c Elementals before barely clenching out a win against Mono-Green Landfall, then got completely destroyed by Izzet Lessons right after. At 9-3, I was almost dead for Top 8, and even a PT qual was looking pretty tough. For my next round, I play against Izzet Spellementals and proceed to play one of the worst games of Magic I've experienced in a while for Game 3: he can't find his second land for two turns but I don't have any colored sources to play any of my spells, so we just sit there doing nothing for a while. He finally finds actions to take before I do and eventually drops a Sunderflock, so I end up losing the match 2-1. At 9-4, I thought I was probably pretty dead for Top 32 and a PT qualification, which felt pretty bad considering the start I had on Day 1.

After the deflating round against Spellementals, I stomped an Izzet Prowess matchup 2-0 (Nova Hellkites pinging off their otters and having a Pyroclasm for their Slickshot + Otter is quite devastating), so I'm now 10-4 heading into the last round. At this point, I'm hearing some mentions of passdowns of PT invites and that there might be a chance for me depending on tiebreaks, but at this point I've mostly come to terms with the fact that I blew the start of the day and am roughly out of contention. But at any rate, we come to the last round and I'm paired into Dimir Midrange: one of my best matchups. I end up winning that matchup 2-0, with my Nova Hellkites doing a lot of work pinging off some Floodpits Drowners/Deep-Cavern Bats. When the standings come out, I'm in 34th with more than 2 passdowns from the top 32, qualifying me for the PT.
