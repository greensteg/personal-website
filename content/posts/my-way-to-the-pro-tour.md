I played my first Pro Tour this past weekend in Las Vegas, and the experience was beyond anything I expected. I finished 19th with an 11-5 record on the weekend: 4-2 in draft, 7-3 in constructed. Along the way, I lost my win-and-in for Worlds. It is hard to complain about a finish like that, but I would be lying if I said I was not at least a little disappointed not to get there in the last match.

The competition was the best I have ever faced: RC and Spotlight winners, Pro Tour winners, and a past World Champion. But instead of starting there, I want to go back to the very beginning of this run, where the dream honestly started with something of a meme.

## Dreamdew Entrancers

Right before my fiancée, Michelle, and I left for a two-month trip to Asia, we registered for the NRG Series Team Trios event at the beginning of November with our friend David. The event was in a suburb of Chicago, so it was easy to get to, and we figured it would be a good time either way.

Michelle played Mono-Red in Standard, before the Screaming Nemesis ban. I played UB Frog in Modern after a friend inspired me to pick up the deck. David, meanwhile, was in the mines playing Spider-Man Sealed. Unfortunately, I punted my face off on Frog for the entire Saturday morning, and we quickly went 1-3, knocking us out of contention for prizes.

David had brought a Modern deck, I had brought a Standard brew, and Michelle still had the Mono-Red deck she had played that morning, so we stuck around to play some ReCQs in the afternoon.

The first thing you should know about me as a Magic player is that I am not afraid to register cards or decks that other people would call "suboptimal." Some of my earliest competitive experiences, back when WotC was taking baby steps toward the Magic Spotlight Series after COVID, were with a Grixis Midrange-slash-combo homebrew featuring [[Hidetsugu and Kairi]] and [[Push // Pull]] in Standard around March of the Machine. The deck was even featured in [this Frank Karsten article|https://magic.gg/news/metagame-mentor-murders-at-karlov-manor-standard-at-magiccon-chicago].

During the ensuing Pioneer RCQ season, I spent an absurd amount of time daydreaming about and testing an Azorius build of [[Nine Lives]] donation in a metagame dominated by Amalia Combo, Izzet Phoenix, and Rakdos Vampires. I could fill another entire essay with the things I have to say about that deck, and I still regret not being able to bring it to an RC.

Anyway, the deck I brought that day was certainly my own creation. In a Standard format where you were allowed to play cards like [[Vivi Ornitier]], [[Proft's Eidetic Memory]], and [[Ouroboroid]], I decided to register this pile of cards:

```deck
Title: Simic Blink
Format: Standard
Source: https://moxfield.com/decks/7EJTECgDk0mitOhjQ8c5Aw

4 Botanical Sanctum
4 Breeding Pool
2 Doppelgang
3 Dreamdew Entrancer
1 Forest
3 Hedge Maze
4 Hide on the Ceiling
2 Into the Flood Maw
3 Island
4 Llanowar Elves
2 Mistrise Village
4 Multiversal Passage
3 Quantum Riddler
1 Restless Vinestalk
3 Spider-Sense
4 Splash Portal
3 Three Steps Ahead
4 Thundertrap Trainer
2 Tishana's Tidebinder
4 Willowrush Verge

Sideboard
1 Aegis Turtle
1 Annul
1 Essence Scatter
1 Ghost Vacuum
2 Heritage Reclamation
1 Into the Flood Maw
1 Negate
1 Pick Your Poison
1 Soul-Guide Lantern
1 Tishana's Tidebinder
1 Ugin, Eye of the Storms
2 Unable to Scream
1 Vivien Reid
```

The deck heavily supported a blink theme with four copies each of [[Splash Portal]] and [[Hide on the Ceiling]]. Both were hits off [[Thundertrap Trainer]], and both generated even more value when blinking Thundertrap Trainer or [[Dreamdew Entrancer]], turning them into a build-your-own Ancestral Recall. They could also produce some explosive starts by scamming [[Quantum Riddler]] into play: with a turn-one dual land into [[Llanowar Elves]], you could put Riddler into play on turn two with a blue land and Splash Portal.

The scam also worked with [[Spider-Sense]], though you did not get your second draw and had to bounce your Llanowar Elves to take that line. In general, Spider-Sense was not just solid interaction; it was also a synergy piece. Bouncing a Thundertrap Trainer or Dreamdew Entrancer for another ETB trigger later felt amazing.

The coolest part of the deck, to me, was the flexibility between the synergy pieces. [[Hide on the Ceiling]] and [[Spider-Sense]] were both blink or self-bounce enablers, but together they were actually a one-sided wrath: target all of the creatures and artifacts you want to exile with Hide on the Ceiling, then Spider-Sense the delayed trigger that would bring them back.

[[Tishana's Tidebinder]] did the same trick when you did not have Spider-Sense, countering the return trigger instead. I played games where I had two copies of Hide on the Ceiling in hand and a Tidebinder on board: I would play one Hide targeting my Tidebinder and the other targeting everything I wanted to exile, then order the triggers so Tidebinder came back first and countered the second return trigger.

[[Dreamdew Entrancer]] was also an important piece of the deck. If you were not under pressure, it could lock down one of your own creatures that you later wanted to bounce with Spider-Sense to draw two. But if you were dying to a large threat, you could just stun it for three turns and buy a huge amount of time.

For finishers, I ended up running two [[Doppelgang]] because the deck drew so many cards and often ended up with a lot of mana when it was time to close. There were certainly other options, and I could have optimized this part more, but it helped that Doppelgang was findable off [[Thundertrap Trainer]]. The sideboard was a bit of everything, though it was mostly geared toward hating on decks with [[Vivi Ornitier]] and giving myself enough time against Mono-Red.

To be honest, I did not think this deck was close to the best deck in Standard at the time. It was abundantly clear that Vivi-based Izzet decks were objectively the best thing to be doing. That said, I loved the lines you could find with the deck, and I loved that you could play severely underestimated cards like [[Dreamdew Entrancer]] and [[Hide on the Ceiling]] while still putting up a real fight against the metagame decks.

I also figured that I would not have a real gameplay edge in the Izzet mirror. I had not played much Standard at that point, and anyone I faced would probably have more reps in those matchups than I did. But by playing this wacky Simic Blink list, I could drag them into the mud on lines that neither of us had much experience with — and maybe I had more experience than they did. This idea of playing a deck you are comfortable piloting as a way to gain equity will resurface later.

My first match in the ReCQ was against Simic Ouroboroid. It was a decent matchup as long as they did not ramp too aggressively. The version they were on played Innkeeper's Talent, which was probably good for me because it made their clock a little slower. I ended up winning 2-0 by assembling the [[Hide on the Ceiling]] plus [[Spider-Sense]] wrath in both games, eating their entire board and leaving them with very little to do.

My second opponent was piloting the villain deck: Izzet Cauldron. In Game 1, I stabilized and found the [[Hide on the Ceiling]] plus [[Tishana's Tidebinder]] wrath, which took their [[Vivi Ornitier]], [[Fear of Missing Out]], and [[Agatha's Soul Cauldron]] — Hide hits artifacts too. Once I stuck a [[Quantum Riddler]], the game ended quickly.

For Game 2, all of my artifact and enchantment hate came in: [[Annul]], [[Heritage Reclamation]], and [[Vivien Reid]]. I had enough answers for his threats, and eventually stuck a Vivien Reid he could not deal with. That engine carried me over the finish line.

Since the ReCQ was small — just eight people — that put me into the finals, where my opponent already had an invite and scooped to me for prizes. Invite acquired with this pile of cards.

## I Really Did Not Want to Play Lessons or Landfall in Milwaukee

Fast-forward a few months. I had a great time in Asia not playing much Magic, aside from a few weekends of Arena Direct, and both TLA and ECL released while I was away. When I arrived back in the US in late January, the Standard metagame had mostly settled around two powerful pillars: Izzet Lessons, with [[Artist's Talent]] and [[Monument to Endurance]], and Simic Cub, with [[Badgermole Cub]] and [[Nature's Rhythm]]. There were also some Four-Color Elementals and Izzet Spellementals builds floating around from PT ECL, with [[Sunderflock]] as a clean answer to Cub.

I had about a month to figure out what to play for RC Milwaukee. I only had one invite and did not want to fly to Portland to play a bunch of LCQs on Friday, but the event crept up on me, and a week out I still had no idea what I was doing.

I had trialed most of the decks a little on MTGA, but none of them felt particularly good to play. Zevin Faust's Azorius Prison list stood out as interesting — many of my friends commented that the list felt very "me," whatever that means — and I picked up a bunch of the cards a few days before the RC.

After more testing on MTGA, though, I realized there were a lot of small decision points with UW Prison that made it incredibly hard to pilot. The deck also had very little card flow besides a couple copies of [[Enduring Curiosity]], so you really had to maximize the value of every card. Honestly, the list looked fun to pilot in theory, but in practice I thought it would give me way too many chances to misplay and punt games away because of my lack of experience. So I decided against it a couple days before the RC.

At that point, I was confused and did not even have many options for acquiring cards before the event. I trawled through decklists on MTGTop8 and MTGGoldfish, looking for any spark of inspiration. After some vigorous digging, I stumbled across the Boros Dragons list that had won the South American RC in Santiago a week or two earlier.

```deck

```

I loaded up the list and played a couple matches on MTGA. I do not remember how the matches went, but I liked how clean the lines felt. Turn-one Momo into turn-two Clarion Conqueror or Nova Hellkite. Sarkhan making a Treasure to power out a Magmatic Hellkite on turn three. The deck had a lot of openings where the curve felt extremely smooth.

I had no delusions that this was the best deck in the format, but it felt powerful on its nut draws and pretty reasonable after sideboarding, with access to white hate pieces and a lot of card advantage from [[The Legend of Roku]] for grindy games.

I also really liked that the deck encouraged you to play spells in your first main phase. [[Momo, Friendly Flier]] and [[Sarkhan, Dragon Ascendant]] both get pumped when your dragons enter, so you are almost always playing creatures before combat, even when they do not have haste. That made sequencing more straightforward in many spots, which gave me less latitude to punt.

Beyond the gameplay, I had played a lot of Tarkir: Dragonstorm Limited. I played the Spotlight Series event in Denver and won five collector booster boxes in the Arena Direct. Michelle convinced me to crack one or two of them on the basis that they were "free" because I had already won back more than my entry on the others. When I sorted through those cards, I found exactly four mismatched [[Sarkhan, Dragon Ascendant]] and more than enough copies of [[Magmatic Hellkite]] and [[Maelstrom of the Spirit Dragon]], which meant I already had most of the list lying around.

With most of the cards ready to go, I ended up registering 74 of the 75 cards from João's winning list, substituting one [[Cavern of Souls]] for [[Multiversal Passage]]. I really dislike [[Multiversal Passage]] in general and often felt like I wanted a [[Cavern of Souls]], especially after sideboard against blue decks, so adding the extra Cavern for three total felt natural.

## Playing More Standard Than I Signed Up For at RC Milwaukee

Going into the tournament, I felt at ease with the outcome, good or bad. As I told my friends repeatedly that weekend, my goal was actually to "minimize the amount of Standard I would be playing" by registering Boros Dragons. I was genuinely excited to play the Limited Championship Qualifier on Sunday after I inevitably failed to make Day 2.

After all, I had played fewer than five matches with the deck entering the tournament, so I had no real expectations. The day before the event, I played some ECL LCQs for fun and spent time finding the last few cards for the tournament. My friends picked up a single [[Dragonhawk, Fate's Tempest]] from The Mana Vault on their way up, and I borrowed a clutch set of [[Requisition Raid]]s from a friend. Somehow, I found all the cards I needed.

The tournament went much better than expected. I started 6-0 and immediately qualified for Day 2, squashing my dreams of playing the LCQ on Sunday.

I was definitely running hot, but I did not feel like I was playing significantly above my usual level. I was, however, playing tight: not throwing games by giving my opponents extra outs, and playing to my own outs when I needed to. I even hit a couple of them. Again, the deck made it easier for me to find the right lines, in part because of the main-phase sequencing incentives I mentioned before.

The other benefit of playing an extremely aggressive deck with no card flow during a long nine-round day was that my rounds were short. I finished every match with around 15 to 20 minutes left on the clock, which gave me plenty of time to decompress, stay hydrated, and even pick up lunch or Gatorade between rounds.

I lost my first round of the day at 6-0 in a tight three-game match against Bant Airbending. The ending sequence left me wondering whether there were ways for me to get there. I recovered quickly afterward and finished the day 8-1.

The strangest part of the entire experience was that I did not feel like I was playing beyond myself. It just felt like a good run with good variance. My friends and I had an excellent dinner and a couple rounds of drinks at [a nice supper club|https://maps.app.goo.gl/BjGRCPvq7xfNeqzU8] for some premature celebration, then ended the night playing a little more ECL Arena Direct and goofing around. Some of them even fired a Commander game before we called it a night.

Day 2 started much worse. I lost badly to Four-Color Elementals, barely clenched out a win against Mono-Green Landfall, then got completely destroyed by Izzet Lessons. At 9-3, I was almost dead for Top 8, and even qualifying for the Pro Tour was starting to look difficult.

In the next round, I played against Izzet Spellementals and proceeded to play one of the worst games of Magic I had experienced in a while. In Game 3, my opponent could not find a second land for two turns, but I had no colored sources to cast any of my spells, so we just sat there doing nothing for a while. Eventually he found action before I did, dropped a Sunderflock, and I lost the match 2-1.

At 9-4, I thought I was probably dead for Top 32 and a Pro Tour qualification, which felt awful considering how Day 1 had started.

After the deflating round against Spellementals, I stomped Izzet Prowess 2-0. Nova Hellkites pinging off their otters and a Pyroclasm for their Slickshot plus Otter draw is quite devastating. That put me at 10-4 heading into the last round.

At that point, I was hearing some mentions of PT invite passdowns and that there might be a chance depending on tiebreaks, but I had mostly come to terms with the fact that I had blown the start of the day and was roughly out of contention.

But in the last round, I was paired into Dimir Midrange, one of my best matchups. I won 2-0, with my Nova Hellkites doing a lot of work pinging off [[Floodpits Drowner]]s and [[Deep-Cavern Bat]]s. When the standings came out, I was in 34th, with more than two passdowns from the Top 32.

I had qualified for the Pro Tour.
